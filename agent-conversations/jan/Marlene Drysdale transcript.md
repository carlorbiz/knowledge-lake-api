This strategic consultation with Marlene Drysdale provided critical insights into rural health workforce challenges, particularly regarding Indigenous health services, registrar support systems, and organisational coordination gaps. Key themes emerged around the need for improved cultural mentoring, enhanced community engagement strategies for workforce retention, and better coordination between training organisations. Marlene highlighted significant concerns about JCTS operational effectiveness while praising RVTS as a model for registrar support. The discussion emphasised opportunities for RWAV to strengthen its coordination role and develop innovative community-driven retention strategies.

Key Strategic Discussion Points
Rural Health Workforce Crisis
Chronic doctor shortages across rural Victoria, particularly in Sale, Gippsland region
High reliance on expensive locum services ($3,500 per day plus accommodation and travel costs)
Local health services pursuing independent recruitment solutions, including international partnerships with Dubai-based agencies
Registrars treating rural placements as temporary requirements rather than career pathways
Community trust and continuity of care compromised by transient workforce model
Cultural Mentoring and Indigenous Health Services
Critical gaps in onboarding, training, and support systems for registrars in Aboriginal Medical Services
Lack of coordinated, universal educational supports for supervisors working in AMS settings
High incidence of bullying between registrars and supervisors with inadequate reporting mechanisms
Need for comprehensive cultural safety training and log/checklist systems for registrar requirements
Particular challenges for overseas-trained doctors from diverse cultural backgrounds entering Indigenous health settings
Organisational Effectiveness Concerns
JCTS criticised as "top-heavy" with excessive management layers and travel expenses
Recruitment challenges for qualified cultural educators due to uncompetitive salary offerings
Inconsistent HR practices and insufficient recruitment standards
Leadership frequently absent due to extensive travel commitments
Organisation expanding beyond original scope without maintaining focus and coordination
Best Practice Models
RVTS identified as exemplary model for registrar and supervisor support
Strong cultural mentorship programs with one-to-one support and designated medical educators
Personal relationship-building and responsive care systems
Respected cultural educators with direct access and appreciation
Successful boot camp programs and webinar series
Community-Driven Solutions
Innovative proposals for social integration including community balls and dating apps for regional doctors
Emphasis on engaging local councils, business councils, and community organisations in workforce attraction
Historical success of community involvement in hooking up medical professionals with local partners
Need to shift from external funding dependency to community-driven initiatives

Integration with Strategic Vision
This consultation reinforces RWAV's strategic positioning as a coordination hub rather than a direct service provider. Marlene's insights highlight the critical need for an organisation that can facilitate partnerships, coordinate training initiatives, and support community-driven solutions. The emphasis on RVTS as a successful model provides a blueprint for scaling effective practices across Victoria.

The discussion aligns with RWAV's values of partnership and collaboration while identifying specific opportunities to enhance connections with First Nations communities through improved cultural safety training and mentorship programs. The focus on community engagement and locally-driven solutions supports RWAV's goal of sustainable workforce development.

Key strategic themes emerging include the importance of relationship-based support systems, the value of coordination over duplication, and the potential for innovative technology solutions to address training and support gaps in rural and Indigenous health settings.

Conclusion
Marlene Drysdale's consultation provided invaluable insights into both the challenges and opportunities within Victoria's rural health workforce landscape. Her extensive experience with various training organisations offers a clear perspective on what works well (RVTS model) versus what requires significant improvement (JCTS operations).

The strategic implications for RWAV are substantial, presenting opportunities to position the organisation as a key coordinator and facilitator of effective workforce solutions. By learning from successful models like RVTS and addressing the coordination gaps highlighted throughout the discussion, RWAV can enhance its impact while maintaining its core focus on partnership and collaboration.

The emphasis on community-driven solutions and the potential for innovative technology applications aligns well with contemporary approaches to rural workforce development while respecting the fundamental importance of cultural safety and Indigenous self-determination in health service delivery.
