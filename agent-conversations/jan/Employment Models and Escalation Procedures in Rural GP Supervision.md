<p class="text-gray-700">Escalation beyond practice level is appropriate when: 1) Internal processes have been exhausted without resolution, 2) The issue involves serious misconduct or patient safety concerns, 3) There is a significant conflict of interest within the practice, 4) The dispute involves interpretation of the NTCER provisions, or 5) The timeframe for resolution at practice level (typically 14 days) has elapsed without progress. Documentation of previous attempts at resolution is essential before external escalation.</p>
</div>
</div>

<div class="faq-item">
<div class="faq-question" onclick="toggleAnswer(this.nextElementSibling)">
<p class="font-medium">What timeframes should practices adhere to in the escalation process?</p>
<i class="fas fa-plus medical-blue text-sm"></i>
</div>
<div class="faq-answer">
<p class="text-gray-700">Key timeframes include: 1) Initial discussion‚Äîwithin 48 hours of issue identification, 2) Practice-level resolution‚Äîattempt within 14 days, 3) Senior management/committee response‚Äîwithin 7 days of escalation, 4) External mediator involvement‚Äîinitiated within 14 days of failed internal resolution, 5) Fair Work Commission referral‚Äîif no resolution within 30 days of initial complaint. These timeframes should be documented in practice policies, with flexibility for complex issues.</p>

<div class="faq-item">
<div class="faq-question" onclick="toggleAnswer(this.nextElementSibling)">
<p class="font-medium">How does the Fair Work Commission process work for GP training disputes?</p>
<i class="fas fa-plus medical-blue text-sm"></i>
</div>
<div class="faq-answer">
<p class="text-gray-700">The FWC process typically involves: 1) Application submission with evidence of prior resolution attempts, 2) Initial conference (usually within 21 days) to explore conciliation options, 3) Mediation/conciliation with appointed FWC member, 4) Formal arbitration if agreed by both parties (resulting in binding decision), 5) Continued workplace operation during the process unless safety issues exist. Either party may appoint a representative, and proceedings are generally less formal than court hearings.</p>

<div class="faq-answer">
