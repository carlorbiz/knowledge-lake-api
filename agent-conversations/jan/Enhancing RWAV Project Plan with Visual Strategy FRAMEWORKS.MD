The Six Paths Framework: Your Strategic North Star
At the heart of transformative strategic thinking lies the Six Paths Framework1, developed by W. Chan Kim and Rene Mauborgne in their groundbreaking book Blue Ocean Strategy. This systematic approach challenges industry assumptions and discovers new market opportunities through six distinct strategic perspectives, each offering a unique lens through which organizations can reimagine their competitive landscape and uncover untapped potential.

The beauty of the Six Paths Framework lies not just in its strategic power, but in its inherent visual nature. Unlike traditional strategic analysis that relies heavily on text-based reports and complex matrices, this framework can be instantly visualized as a simple "bird's-eye view" diagram of six interconnected paths, each representing a different strategic perspective to explore.

The six paths are: 1. Look Across Alternative Industries, 2. Look Across Strategic Groups Within Industries, 3. Look Across the Chain of Buyers, 4. Path 1: Look Across Alternative Industries
The first path challenges organizations to expand their vision beyond traditional industry boundaries. Rather than competing within established industry lines, this approach identifies alternative industries that serve similar customer needs through different means. The visual representation of this path typically shows interconnected industry circles with customer needs at the center, demonstrating how different industries can address the same fundamental requirements.

For example, when Southwest Airlines looked across alternative industries, they discovered that their competition wasn't just other airlines, but also buses, trains, and car travel. By visualizing this insight through comparative journey maps and cost-benefit analyses, they created a new market space that combined the speed of air travel with the simplicity and affordability of bus transportation.

This path's visual documentation often employs cross-industry comparison matrices that plot different industries against key customer value dimensions, revealing opportunities to combine the best attributes from multiple industries into innovative value propositions.

Path 2: Look Across Strategic Groups Within Industries
This path focuses on the strategic groups that exist within any industry‚Äîclusters of companies that pursue similar strategies. Visual mapping techniques4 for this path often include positioning matrices that plot companies based on key strategic dimensions like price versus service, showing gaps between strategic groups that represent potential blue ocean opportunities.

The most effective visual representations of strategic group analysis use bubble charts or scatter plots where the size and position of each bubble represents different strategic dimensions, making it immediately apparent where opportunities exist between established groups. These visualizations reveal white spaces in the competitive landscape that traditional industry analysis might miss.

Path 3: Look Across the Chain of Buyers
The third path examines the entire chain of buyers involved in purchasing decisions, from influencers to users to purchasers. This framework recognizes that in most industries, there's a chain of "buyers" who are involved directly or indirectly in the buying decision, and that by focusing on a previously neglected buyer group, companies can unlock new value.

Visual documentation for this path typically employs buyer journey maps and stakeholder influence diagrams that clearly show the relationships and decision-making power of different participants in the purchasing process. These visual tools make it instantly apparent which buyer groups have been overlooked or underserved, revealing opportunities for strategic repositioning.

Path 4: Look Across Complementary Product and Service Offerings
This path encourages examination of complementary products and services that are used alongside an organization's core offering. By understanding the total solution that buyers seek, companies can identify opportunities to expand their value proposition by addressing pain points in complementary areas.

Effective visual communication for this path often includes ecosystem maps that show the complete customer journey and all touchpoints, services, and products involved in achieving the customer's ultimate goal. These comprehensive visual maps reveal opportunities for integration and value creation that may not be apparent in traditional product-focused analysis.

Path 5: Look Across Functional or Emotional Appeal to Buyers
The fifth path explores the possibility of changing an industry's traditional appeal to buyers‚Äîshifting from functional to emotional appeal or vice versa. Some industries compete primarily on function, while others compete on emotion. By identifying and reversing the industry's dominant appeal, companies can discover new market spaces.

Visual representation of this path often employs emotional journey mapping alongside functional benefit analysis, creating side-by-side comparisons that clearly illustrate opportunities to differentiate through appeal transformation. Infographics that contrast emotional and functional benefits can make these insights immediately graspable for strategic decision-makers.

Path 6: Look Across Time
The final path encourages organizations to look across time by participating in shaping external trends that impact their business. Rather than simply reacting to trends, companies can help shape them by identifying the decisive trends that are irreversible and have clear business impact.
