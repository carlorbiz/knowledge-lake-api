## Research Foundation for Clinical Supervision and Professional Development for International Medical Graduates

This research foundation establishes the credibility and evidence base for a professional development micro-credential course aimed at experienced clinicians supervising International Medical Graduates (IMGs) in the Australian healthcare context, particularly focusing on clinical supervision and cultural integration.

### Healthcare Core Instructions and Quality Standards Alignment

As experts in Australian healthcare professional development and clinical supervision, this foundation ensures that content is **evidence-based** and grounded exclusively in the provided source materials. The focus remains on the **Australian healthcare context and regulatory compliance**, targeting experienced clinicians with **clear, actionable guidance** for immediate implementation. Crucially, the content addresses **cultural safety and diversity considerations** and incorporates **adult learning principles** appropriate for workplace application.

**Course Concept:** Clinical Supervision and Professional Development for International Medical Graduates (IMGs) in the Australian Healthcare Context.
**Target Audience:** Experienced Clinicians and GP Supervisors.

## 1. SOURCE ANALYSIS SUMMARY

The body of evidence reviewed establishes that International Medical Graduates (IMGs) are essential to the Australian healthcare system, constituting over 25% of the national medical workforce and often exceeding 30% in rural and remote areas. Supporting the successful transition and retention of IMGs is vital for rural community benefit. Training IMGs is acknowledged as uniquely challenging, often differing significantly from educating domestic graduates.

### Challenges in IMG Integration

The research highlights key tensions and obstacles that IMGs and their supervisors face, falling primarily into communication, cultural transition, and systemic integration issues:

**1. Communication and Consultation Style:**
IMGs frequently experience challenges related to communication that extend beyond basic English proficiency. Although many IMGs achieve the minimum International English Language Testing System (IELTS) score of 7, this band descriptor allows for "occasional inaccuracies, inappropriacies and misunderstandings". Communication problems exist at a "subtle level," including the need to read non-verbal cues and respond with cultural appropriateness. Many IMGs report difficulty expressing caring and empathy in a new cultural context. IMGs often struggle with **jargon, colloquialisms, and regional dialects**, which can impair their ability to understand patients and colleagues.

**2. Patient-Centred Care (PCC) and Professional Identity:**
A major challenge is the shift from the IMG’s country of origin, where medical systems are often paternalistic, doctor-dominated, and rely on patient compliance and trust. This contrasts with the Australian health system, which is consumer-oriented and demands an equitable doctor-patient relationship, requiring high levels of information and discussion.
IMGs unfamiliar with PCC often exhibit "novice" communication strategies, such as framing consultations as highly structured interviews (rather than conversations), maintaining strict topic control, and failing to establish comprehensive coherence in interactions. IMGs may function as **'expert novices,'** meaning they are biomedical experts but novices in PCC communication skills. The struggle to adopt PCC can stem from deep-seated cultural differences, making it hard to discuss delicate personal or psychosocial matters.

**3. Regulatory and Systemic Navigation:**
IMGs must rapidly grasp Australian health system protocols, clinical practice organization, and regulatory compliance requirements. IMGs often request additional information regarding the **Australian healthcare system** and **Indigenous health**. Financial and billing topics (Medicare items, prescribing, medicolegal issues) are rated as highly important by IMGs, yet confidence remains low in areas like special Medicare payments and medicolegal issues after orientation. Furthermore, navigating complex administrative burdens related to visas, registration (AHPRA), and assessments (AMC, PESCI) is costly, time-consuming, and psychologically stressful.

**4. Learning Styles and Supervision Needs:**
Many IMGs originate from medical education systems that are teacher-centred, lecture-focused, and based on memorization. They may find Western experiential learning, problem-based discussion, or reflective practice difficult, sometimes interpreting candid feedback as personal criticism ("loss of face"). Supervisors often find that IMGs need targeted support to apply their clinical knowledge to common Australian general practice presentations (e.g., childhood asthma, depression).

### Evidence-Based Solutions and Support

To overcome these challenges, the research points towards the need for **flexible, individualized, and high-quality support** systems:

*   **Integrated Support:** While targeted initiatives are necessary, results support integrating IMG support into the broader system to support all doctors, viewing IMGs as an integral part of the workforce.
*   **Structured Information and Orientation:** Providing clear, centralised information about the entire pathway (AHPRA, AMC, colleges) is crucial. Orientation should include Australian culture, community facilities, and support for the IMG's family. IMGs need **individualized advice** to interpret and apply these rules.
*   **Quality Supervision:** Supervisors must be culturally aware and equipped with skills to support IMGs. This includes promoting a culture of psychological safety where IMGs feel comfortable asking questions without fear of being judged incompetent or risking their registration. Supervision requires resources and funding to facilitate reflective practice and regular, specific feedback.
*   **Assessment and Time:** Educators note that many IMGs require longer time in training to achieve qualifications. Training should implement more emphasis on **progressive formative assessment** (like workplace-based assessment or WBA) rather than summative assessments, which reduces anxiety and facilitates timely feedback.

## 2. KEY FRAMEWORKS AND METHODOLOGIES IDENTIFIED

**Evidence-based frameworks from sources:**

*   **Patient-Centred Care (PCC):** A model focusing on six core components: exploring disease and illness experience, understanding the whole person, finding common ground, incorporating prevention, enhancing the patient-doctor relationship, and being realistic.
*   **Quality of Discourse Model (Candlin):** A framework used to evaluate professional communicative expertise based on: **Framing** (interview vs. conversation), **Topic Management** (structured checklist vs. flexibility), and **Coherence** (simple vs. comprehensive links).
*   **The 'Expert Novice' Construct:** A conceptual framework recognizing that IMGs possess high biomedical expertise but may be novice in the required socio-cultural competencies, particularly PCC.
*   **Calgary-Cambridge Guides:** Recommended framework for introducing IMGs to communication styles in Western medical systems.
*   **Three-Stage Roadmap for IMG Support (GPSA 2025):** Divides the IMG journey into: Stage 1. Arriving in a new country and acclimatizing (Aim: Comfortable and Empowered); Stage 2. Moving to new workplaces and communities (Aim: Confidence, Competency, and Community Belonging); and Stage 3. Training to become a specialist GP (Aim: Professional Belonging and Bonding).

**Australian Healthcare Standards and Guidelines:**

*   **Australian Medical Council (AMC) Examinations:** The formal process for assessing IMGs for general registration or specialist pathway entry, which often serves as a primary motivation and pressure point for IMGs.
*   **Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency (AHPRA):** Regulatory body overseeing registration requirements, including provisional status, limited registration, and approval of supervised practice plans for Specialist IMGs (SIMGs).
*   **College Requirements (RACGP/ACRRM):** Professional standards and pathways leading to fellowship, requiring clinical placements and examinations.
*   **Therapeutic Guidelines/Australian Medical Handbook:** Essential resources that IMGs need training and guidance to use effectively in the local context.

**Professional Development Methodologies:**

*   **Individualized and Flexible Training:** Programs must move away from standardized curricula to tailor support based on early assessment of individual strengths and weaknesses, allowing for adjustment of training time and content.
*   **Hub and Spoke Model:** A structural approach where a central coordinating office designs the curriculum and on-site clinical educators implement the programs and provide direct support.
*   **Direct Observation and Workplace-Based Assessment (WBA):** Preferred formative methods for assessing practical skills, communication, and competence in a less anxiety-inducing environment compared to summative exams.
*   **Reflection and Mentorship:** Utilizing funded supervision that promotes reflective practice alongside access to early, independent career mentors and real-time pastoral care for complex challenges.

## 3. KEY CONCEPTS AND EVIDENCE-BASED INSIGHTS

**Critical Success Factors for Australian Healthcare Professionals (IMGs):**

*   **Equitable Doctor-Patient Relationship:** Success hinges on the IMG's ability to transition from a hierarchical consultation model to an equitable one, where patients actively participate and question care.
*   **Holistic Communication Skills:** Mastering "soft skills" like professional identity, empathy, reflective listening, and rapport-building is essential, as deficiencies in these areas contribute significantly to challenges.
*   **Integration Mindset:** IMGs who are satisfied and contented with their current practice and life in a rural setting intend to remain significantly longer (11.5–11.8 years) than those who are dissatisfied (7.8–8.2 years). This underscores the critical link between professional support, community belonging, and retention.
*   **Recognizing Prior Expertise:** Programs must acknowledge and build upon the diverse experience and specialist qualifications IMGs bring, potentially fast-tracking certain areas where competence is demonstrated.

**Evidence Gaps and Opportunities:**

*   **Supervision Power Differential:** A significant gap is the difficulty for IMGs to report inadequate supervision when their supervisor is also their employer, creating a massive conflict of interest and power differential. This mandates the need for external oversight, independent audits, and a confidential helpline/exit strategy.
*   **Pre-Practice Support:** A lack of structured, remunerated observerships or IMG preparedness programs within an indemnity framework leaves IMGs learning complex system navigation and practical skills (like completing referrals or using software) on the job, leading to unnecessary stress and risk.
*   **Addressing Language/Metropolitan Bias in Assessment:** There is a persistent risk of language and metropolitan bias in training assessments, particularly affecting rural IMGs who deal with a broader caseload and unique regional issues.

**Implementation Insights for Professional Development:**

*   **Early Focus on Systems/Culture:** Training must front-load information on Australian health systems (including allied health roles), cultural norms, and professional conduct, ideally during pre-work orientation or preparation periods.
*   **Structured Mentorship and Peer Networks:** Implementation should mandate early access to independent GP career mentors, and facilitate IMG-focused peer support networks for safe sharing of experiences and pastoral care.
*   **Family-Centred Implementation:** Recognizing that IMGs often arrive with limited social support and significant family commitments, training must be flexible and incorporate support for spouses and children to integrate into the community. Professional events should invite families to foster a sense of belonging.

## 4. REGULATORY AND COMPLIANCE CONSIDERATIONS (Australian context)

**AHPRA Standards and Requirements:**
The Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency (AHPRA) dictates the registration requirements, which often involve limited registration subject to supervised practice. The Medical Board must approve the supervised practice plan, which determines where a SIMG can practice. IMGs require clarity and justification regarding visa and registration requirements, as delays or rejections (e.g., Change of Circumstances applications) can severely impact their capacity to earn a living. Supervisors play a critical role in guiding IMGs through complex documentation requirements.

**GP College Guidelines (RACGP/ACRRM) where relevant:**
IMGs striving for general practice fellowship must meet the standards of the Australian College of Rural and Remote Medicine (ACRRM) or the Royal Australian College of General Practitioners (RACGP). The colleges manage various pathways (e.g., AGPT, FSP). Training programs need to align with college objectives, particularly recognizing the importance of continuity of training in one location where appropriate to support family life and settlement.

**NSQHS Standards Alignment:**
Course material must ensure alignment with the overarching goals of patient safety and quality improvement. This includes providing functional instruction on clinical governance elements like patient handover, early notification of senior staff, and working effectively within interdisciplinary teams. The ultimate objective is to produce "fit for purpose doctors" providing "safe, quality care".

**Cultural Safety Requirements:**
Specific regulatory and professional development obligations require addressing cultural safety. This includes mandatory education for IMGs on Australian and Indigenous culture. Supervisors and staff must build capacity to demonstrate cultural safety and provide pastoral care, deepening their understanding of IMGs’ unique experiences and strengths. The concept of the course itself, being culturally appropriate and inclusive, fulfills a compliance requirement.

**Professional Development Obligations:**
Experienced clinicians have an obligation to provide and engage in training that addresses key professional priority issues, including continuing professional development and training opportunities. Clinicians training IMGs specifically need skills in delivering feedback and understanding the impact of different teaching systems.

## 5. CONTENT SUMMARIES BY SOURCE (Vancouver-style citations)

**Carlier N, Carlier M, Bisset G. Orientation of IMGs: a rural evaluation. *Aust Fam Physician* 2005;34(6):485–7.**
*   **Key evidence points:** The evaluation found that IMGs prioritized clinical and financial topics (e.g., prescribing, medicolegal issues, billing) during orientation. However, confidence was lowest in special Medicare payments, medicolegal issues, and Aboriginal issues after orientation. Orientation lasted a median of 1 week, though 2 weeks was preferred, continuing for 1 week after consultation began. Dissatisfaction with orientation was higher (60%) for the 25% of doctors who received no formal orientation. Suggested improvements included ensuring income was not compromised during orientation and providing an emergency medicine update.
*   **Relevance to course concept:** Provides quantifiable evidence of specific knowledge gaps (medicolegal, Aboriginal issues) and preferred orientation duration in a rural setting.
*   **Limitations and considerations:** Small sample (n=20) from rural South Australia in 2005; results reflect a regional, historical context.

**Wearne SM, Brown JB, Kirby C, Snadden D. International medical graduates and general practice training: How do educational leaders facilitate the transition from new migrant to local family doctor? *Med Teach* 2019;DOI:10.1080/0142159X.2019.1616681.**
*   **Key evidence points:** IMGs’ differing educational needs benefit from flexible, individualized training adaptation. Educators feel an ethical obligation to support IMGs to provide quality health care. Tensions arise because teaching IMGs can differ from teaching domestic graduates across all program aspects. Differences include communication, understanding systems, and professionalism. Solutions identified include clear orientation, battling prejudice, direct observation, monitoring, and recognizing that IMGs often need longer in training.
*   **Relevance to course concept:** Establishes the necessity for flexible, individualized training models and highlights the broad scope of differences (systemic, soft skills, educational styles) that supervisors must address.
*   **Limitations and considerations:** Qualitative study based on the perspective of 28 leaders of GP training from six countries.

**Dahm MR. Patient centred care - are international medical graduates 'expert novices'? *Aust Fam Physician* 2011;40(11):895–900.**
*   **Key evidence points:** Many IMGs are unfamiliar with PCC, having trained in science-focused medical curricula. IMGs observed in role plays adopted novice discourse features, such as framing consultations as interviews, maintaining strict topic control, and achieving simple coherence. The difficulty stems from being 'expert novices' (biomedical experts but communication novices). Unfamiliarity with Australian culture contributes to communication difficulties, particularly when addressing sensitive personal matters.
*   **Relevance to course concept:** Defines PCC as a crucial skill gap and introduces the 'expert novice' concept, which should inform training design.

**Pilotto LS, Duncan GF, Anderson-Wurf J. Issues for clinicians training international medical graduates: a systematic review. *Med J Aust* 2007;187(4):225–8.**
*   **Key evidence points:** IMGs face challenges adjusting to a change in status (moving from highly regarded doctor to "student"). Clinicians need to develop IMGs’ skills in subtle and pragmatic aspects of language interaction, including reading non-verbal cues and developing rapport. IMGs often expect didactic teaching and may interpret negative feedback as personal criticism. Clinicians require specific training resources (workshops, online modules) to address these issues effectively.
*   **Relevance to course concept:** Systematic review identifying five critical themes for training clinicians. Provides actionable advice for supervisors on teaching styles (e.g., using open-ended questions) and feedback delivery.

**McGrath P, Henderson D, Tamargo J, Holewa H. Doctor-patient communication issues for international medical graduates: research findings from Australia. *Educ Health* 2012;25(1):48-54.**
*   **Key evidence points:** Understanding patient-centred communication (PCC) is a major challenge due to the shift from paternalistic systems (where doctors often talk to the family, not the patient). Australian patients are viewed as more educated, informed, and demanding of information, making consultations time-consuming. IMGs strongly perceive the need for education on PCC, either pre-practice or ongoing.
*   **Relevance to course concept:** Provides qualitative evidence from an Australian hospital confirming that PCC adoption is a significant professional and cultural challenge that requires structured education.

**Couser G. Twelve tips for developing training programs for international medical graduates. *Med Teach* 2007;29(5):427-30.**
*   **Key evidence points:** Programs should adopt a 'hub and spoke' model, involving a central coordinating office and on-site clinical educators. Key recommendations include understanding IMGs' prior experience, accommodating cross-cultural learning styles (e.g., didactic preference), relating training to safety and quality, engaging existing staff, and providing holistic support for the IMG's family. Training should use frameworks like the Calgary-Cambridge guides for communication skills.
*   **Relevance to course concept:** Offers practical, high-level guidance for structuring and implementing professional development programs for IMGs, emphasizing quality assurance and cultural sensitivity.

**Alexander C, Fraser JD. Education, training and support needs of Australian trained doctors and international medical graduates in rural Australia: a case of special needs? *Rural Remote Health* 2007;7(Online):681.**
*   **Key evidence points:** IMGs and Australian-trained doctors in rural NSW largely agree on key professional needs: continuing professional development, training opportunities, and professional support. High levels of professional satisfaction correlate strongly with intended long-term retention in rural practice (11.5–11.8 years). The results support an integrated approach, suggesting that IMG initiatives should be part of the broader system to support all rural doctors.
*   **Relevance to course concept:** Provides quantitative evidence that training and support initiatives are high-priority needs for the entire rural GP workforce, justifying the professional development focus.

**McDonnell L, Usherwood T. International medical graduates: challenges faced in the Australian training program. *Aust Fam Physician* 2008;37(6):481-4.**
*   **Key evidence points:** Identified six core challenges: language/communication, cultural issues, understanding the Australian health system, clinical knowledge/application, consulting styles, and registrar support. Challenges persisted even for IMGs long settled in Australia (11–17 years). Supervisors noted that IMGs often miss non-verbal cues and may prefer a paternalistic consulting style. Registrar support issues include financial stress and isolation, especially during rural terms.
*   **Relevance to course concept:** Provides specific themes and qualitative context for training needs within the Australian GP training program, emphasizing supervisor cultural awareness training.

**O’Sullivan B, Omond K, Sedaghat N. IMGs from arrival to fellowship: A roadmap to support IMGs as a valued part of Australia’s rural GP workforce. General Practice Supervision Australia; 2025.**
*   **Key evidence points:** Proposes a three-stage roadmap focusing on progression through comfort/empowerment, confidence/competency/belonging, and professional belonging/bonding. Strategies emphasize providing **clear, centralized, and individualized information** on migration, AHPRA/AMC processes, and Australian life. Critical areas include ensuring **supportive workplaces** (structured matching, graduated caseloads), providing **skills bridging** (GP-specific skills, First Nations health, Australian culture), and implementing **equitable training** (more formative assessment, recognition of experience, family-focused options). Highlights the critical need for **independent audit of supervision quality** due to the power differential.
*   **Relevance to course concept:** Provides the most contemporary and comprehensive roadmap for structuring support, emphasizing systemic solutions, family integration, and quality/equity in training.

## 6. RESEARCH SYNTHESIS FOR COURSE DEVELOPMENT

### How Research Informs Course Architecture

The course must adopt an architecture that supports the IMG across the entire integration spectrum, recognizing the transition from "expert novice" to independent professional.

1.  **Staged and Modular Approach:** The content should be structured along the continuum of the IMG experience: initial system orientation, followed by clinical integration skills, and finally, advanced supervision/training navigation, aligning with the "Roadmap" stages.
2.  **Flexible Delivery Model:** Given that IMGs may arrive sporadically and supervisors operate under time constraints in isolated rural areas, the course should be delivered using flexible methods, such as a **"Hub and Spoke" model**, combining centralized expert content with on-site, practical application and peer discussion.
3.  **Focus on Application:** Content must incorporate adult learning principles, emphasizing experiential learning, reflection, and practical case studies rather than didactic instruction, especially for complex non-clinical skills like professionalism and communication.

### Evidence-Based Module Recommendations

Modules should be designed to bridge the gaps identified in the research:

1.  **Navigating the Australian System and Regulatory Context (Systemic Integration):**
    *   Content must provide clarity on Australian healthcare structure, referral pathways, billing mechanics (MBS/PBS), medicolegal requirements, and the role of allied health professionals.
    *   Training for supervisors must include mechanisms for ensuring IMGs receive individualized guidance on complex AHPRA/AMC requirements and combatting initial financial/administrative stress.
2.  **Mastering Patient-Centred and Culturally Safe Communication:**
    *   This module must teach the shift from physician-centred to equitable, consumer-oriented communication.
    *   Instruction should target and correct specific novice errors (e.g., strict topic control, interview framing) using the Quality of Discourse model.
    *   Content must explicitly cover **First Nations health and cultural safety**, as well as how to read subtle non-verbal cues, manage colloquialisms, and express empathy in a culturally appropriate way.
3.  **Best Practice Clinical Supervision and Feedback:**
    *   Training must equip supervisors to conduct **early, objective assessments** of capacity/knowledge and develop tailored learning plans.
    *   Supervisors need skills in promoting psychological safety, encouraging IMGs to ask questions without fear, and managing the power differential inherent when supervisors are also employers.
    *   Focus on delivering **regular, specific, and constructive feedback** that promotes reflective practice while guarding against criticism being perceived as "loss of face".

### Assessment Strategy Implications

The course assessment strategy must promote learning equity and confidence building:

*   **Prioritize Formative, Low-Stakes Assessment:** Reliance on high-stakes, summative examinations is counterproductive to confidence and competency building. Assessment should emphasize **progressive formative tools**, such as structured observation, reflective tasks, and case-based discussions.
*   **Assessment of Contextual Skills:** Practical skills (PCC, clinical reasoning, application of knowledge to Australian presentations) should be assessed via practical application (e.g., role-play debriefs, simulation, or WBA tools).
*   **Audit for Bias:** Assessments must be designed and audited to ensure they are free from metropolitan or language biases that disproportionately affect IMGs on rural pathways.

### Implementation Guidance

Successful course implementation relies on creating a robust support ecosystem:

*   **Supervisor Cultural Capacity Building:** Implementation must include targeted training for domestic healthcare and agency staff to deepen their understanding of IMGs’ strengths, experiences, and needs, thereby fostering collegiality and cultural safety.
*   **Mandated Peer and Mentor Networks:** Establish formal mechanisms for IMGs to access independent career mentors and peer support networks (IMG to IMG) to combat professional and social isolation, particularly in rural settings.
*   **Addressing Power Dynamics:** Training organizations must resource the provision of independent external oversight and dedicated resources (like a helpline and change-of-circumstance support) to ensure IMGs are safe and protected in situations of poor or unsafe supervision.
*   **Family Inclusion:** Implementation should actively invite and encourage IMG families to professional events and workshops to foster bonding and community belonging, recognizing that holistic support improves retention.
